## 1. Hilbert's 23 Problems (1900) — Map of 20th-century Mathematics

### 1.1 Background of the Announcement
- German mathematician **David Hilbert** (1862–1943) announced a list of unsolved mathematical problems in his lecture "Mathematische Probleme" at the 2nd International Congress of Mathematicians (ICM Paris 1900) on August 8, 1900.
- The lecture directly introduced 10 problems; the printed proceedings (*Göttinger Nachrichten*, 1900; *Archiv der Mathematik und Physik*, 1901) contained all 23.
- Hilbert's intent: set the direction of 20th-century mathematics, share problem awareness across the international community. The famous slogan of the lecture, "We must know — we shall know" (Wir müssen wissen, wir werden wissen), later became his epitaph.

### 1.2 Progress over 100 Years — Selected Solved Cases
Hilbert's 23 problems cover a very wide range of fields, and progress over 100 years has been uneven. Representative solved/partially-solved cases include:

- **Problem 1 (Continuum Hypothesis)**: Kurt Gödel (1940, continuum hypothesis is consistent with ZFC) + Paul Cohen (1963, its negation is also consistent with ZFC, forcing) = **independent from ZFC**. In the classical sense, rather than "solved" this is "undecidability demonstrated."
- **Problem 2 (Consistency of arithmetic)**: Gödel's incompleteness theorems (1931) shattered the original Hilbert-program dream of a strict finitary proof. Gentzen (1936) showed that consistency of PA is provable if transfinite induction is permitted.
- **Problem 3 (Volume decomposition)**: Max Dehn (1900) **solved** it the same year using Dehn invariants (negative answer). General 3-dimensional polyhedra cannot be transformed into each other by finite piece decomposition.
- **Problem 5 (Analytic structure of Lie groups)**: Gleason, Montgomery, Zippin (ca. 1952) **solved**. Continuous groups are automatically analytic (locally Euclidean topological groups are Lie groups).
- **Problem 7 (Transcendental numbers)**: Gelfond–Schneider (1934) **solved**. If a ≠ 0, 1 is algebraic and b is irrational algebraic, then a^b is transcendental.
- **Problem 10 (Diophantine-equation decision algorithm)**: Yuri Matiyasevich (1970) **solved** (negative answer). MRDP theorem (Matiyasevich–Robinson–Davis–Putnam) — no general Diophantine-solving algorithm exists.
- **Problem 13 (Decomposition of a general 7th-degree equation as compositions of continuous 2-variable functions)**: Kolmogorov–Arnold (1957) **solved** (positive in the continuous sense). The subtlety (continuous vs analytic) of the original question alters the answer.
- **Problem 14 (Finite generation of invariant rings)**: Nagata (1958) — **negative answer** in specific cases (counterexample construction).
- **Problem 17 (Representing positive rational functions as sums of squares)**: Artin (1927) **solved** (positive).
- **Problem 18 (Space-filling problem, Kepler conjecture)**: Hales (1998 announcement, formal formalization completed 2014) — proved the Kepler conjecture. The original Problem 18 included several sub-questions such as crystallographic-group classification; some of these were solved by Bieberbach (1910).
- **Problems 19, 20 (Analyticity/existence of solutions to variational problems)**: De Giorgi, Nash (1957–1958) **solved**. Regularity theory for linear elliptic PDEs.

- **Representative problems still open**:
  - **Problem 6** (axiomatization of physics) — scope is so wide it remains in progress. The Yang-Mills mass gap is sometimes regarded as a modern successor to Problem 6.
  - **Problem 8** (Riemann hypothesis + Goldbach + Twin prime) — unresolved.
  - **Problem 12** (Kronecker's Jugendtraum, abelian extensions over algebraic number fields) — resolved for imaginary quadratic fields via CM theory; general fields still open. Progenitor of the Langlands program.
  - **Problem 16** (topology of algebraic curves and vector fields) — unresolved.

### 1.3 Legacy of Hilbert's 23 Problems
- Served to map the whole field of 20th-century mathematics. Many mathematicians treated "solving one of Hilbert's problems" as the pinnacle of their careers.
- Clay's selection of seven problems in 2000 was explicitly aimed at the centennial of Hilbert.

## 2. Founding of the Clay Mathematics Institute

### 2.1 Landon T. Clay and CMI
- **Landon T. Clay** (1926–2017) was a Boston-based American mutual-fund manager and businessman. Not trained in mathematics, but he took an interest in it as a Harvard undergraduate and in his later years donated from his private funds to promote mathematics.
- **Clay Mathematics Institute (CMI)**: Founded in September 1998 in Cambridge, Massachusetts by Landon T. Clay and his wife Lavinia D. Clay as a nonprofit private foundation. Currently its official address is Peterborough, New Hampshire.
- Founding purpose (official): "to increase and disseminate mathematical knowledge." Operates Clay Research Fellowships, public lectures, publications, etc.

### 2.2 Scientific Advisory Board
- The Scientific Advisory Board at CMI's founding consisted of the world's leading mathematicians at the time.
- **Arthur Jaffe** (Harvard, mathematical physics, initial CMI president role), **Andrew Wiles** (Princeton, prover of Fermat's Last Theorem), **Alain Connes** (IHÉS/Collège de France, noncommutative geometry), **Edward Witten** (IAS, mathematical physics/string theory), **John Tate** (Harvard/Texas, algebraic number theory), and others officially served as advisors.
- Over two years (1998–2000) of discussions, the final list of "seven great problems" was finalized.

### 2.3 Selection Criteria (Clay official statement)
According to the CMI official announcement and Jaffe's (2006) recollection, the selection criteria can be summarized as three:
1. **Old and fundamental**: already withstood decades of attempts by top mathematicians, with the answer at the center of its discipline.
2. **Representative of the whole discipline**: a solution should not be a one-off result but should determine the direction of its field.
3. **Cross-disciplinary connection**: not confined to pure mathematics internally, but carrying influence into physics, computer science, etc.

### 2.4 May 24, 2000 Paris Announcement at Collège de France
- **Date**: May 24, 2000
- **Venue**: Collège de France, Paris
- **Format**: A public lecture explicitly aimed at the centennial of Hilbert. John Tate (Riemann hypothesis, BSD), Michael Atiyah (other problems), etc. each presented.
- **Prize**: US$ 1,000,000 per problem, total US$ 7,000,000.
- **Award rules**: Solutions must be published in peer-reviewed journals and then subjected to two years of verification by the international mathematical community. Because of this rule, despite Perelman's 2002–2003 arXiv preprints, the award decision was not made until 2010 (and even then Perelman declined).

### 3.2 One-paragraph Summary of Each Problem

**(1) Riemann Hypothesis (1859)**
- **Source**: Bernhard Riemann, "Über die Anzahl der Primzahlen unter einer gegebenen Grösse" (On the number of primes less than a given magnitude), Monatsberichte der Berliner Akademie, November 1859.
- **Question**: For the Riemann zeta function ζ(s) = Σ_{n≥1} n^{-s} (extended to s ∈ ℂ by analytic continuation), do all **non-trivial zeros** (zeros other than s = -2, -4, -6, …) lie exactly on the critical line with real part 1/2?
- **Importance**: Implies the error term O(x^{1/2+ε}) for the prime-counting function π(x) versus the logarithmic integral Li(x). A pillar of all analytic number theory.
- **Current status**: Numerically, more than 10^{13} non-trivial zeros have been confirmed on the critical line (Odlyzko, Platt, etc.). Theoretically **unresolved**.

**(2) P vs NP (1971)**
- **Source**: Stephen Cook, "The complexity of theorem-proving procedures", Proceedings of the 3rd Annual ACM Symposium on Theory of Computing (STOC 1971); Leonid Levin (1973, independent proposal, in Russian); Richard Karp (1972, 21 NP-complete problems).
- **Question**: For every decision problem whose solutions can be **verified** in polynomial time, can a solution also be **found** in polynomial time? I.e., does P = NP?
- **Importance**: Most of the modern computational world — public-key cryptography, optimization, AI/ML limits, automated theorem proving — hinges on the answer. Most expect P ≠ NP but the proof tools are lacking.
- **Current status**: **unresolved**. Major lower-bound techniques (diagonalization, the natural-proofs barrier Razborov-Rudich 1997, the algebraic barrier, etc.) block every known simple approach.

**(3) Yang-Mills Existence and Mass Gap (Clay formal question, 2000)**
- **Source**: C. N. Yang · R. L. Mills, "Conservation of Isotopic Spin and Isotopic Gauge Invariance", Physical Review 96, 191 (1954). Clay formal formulation: Arthur Jaffe · Edward Witten, "Quantum Yang-Mills Theory", 2000 (Clay official document).
- **Question**: Prove that **quantum Yang-Mills theory** for an arbitrary compact simple gauge group G (e.g., SU(2), SU(3)) on 4-dimensional Euclidean space ℝ^4 **exists in a mathematically rigorous sense** — satisfying the Wightman axioms or the Osterwalder-Schrader axioms — and prove a **mass gap Δ > 0** (i.e., the minimum excitation above the vacuum is separated from zero).
- **Importance**: Mathematical explanation of color confinement in the strong interaction (QCD). Mathematical foundation of the Standard Model of modern particle physics.
- **Current status**: **unresolved**. There are lattice gauge theory, Osterwalder-Schrader, and partial 2-/3-dimensional results (Glimm-Jaffe, etc.), but rigorous existence in 4 dimensions remains open.

**(4) Navier-Stokes Existence and Smoothness (Clay 2000)**
- **Source**: The equations themselves are due to Claude-Louis Navier (1822) · George Gabriel Stokes (1845). Clay official document: Charles Fefferman, "Existence and Smoothness of the Navier-Stokes Equation", 2000.
- **Question**: When the initial velocity field is smooth (defined on all of ℝ^3) and decays fast enough at infinity, prove that the 3D incompressible Navier-Stokes equations admit a solution existing and smooth for all time, or construct a counterexample with a finite-time singularity.
- **Importance**: Foundation of fluid dynamics (atmosphere, ocean, aerodynamics). Leray's (1934) weak solutions partially solve existence, but regularity and uniqueness remain unresolved.
- **Current status**: **unresolved**. Leray-Hopf weak-solution existence, Scheffer (1976) · Caffarelli-Kohn-Nirenberg (1982) partial regularity, Tao's mean-field-approximation blow-up construction (2016), etc. are partial.

**(5) Hodge Conjecture (1950)**
- **Source**: William V. D. Hodge, "The topological invariants of algebraic varieties", Proceedings of the International Congress of Mathematicians (ICM Cambridge, Mass.) 1950, vol. 1, 182–192.
- **Question**: On a smooth projective complex algebraic variety X, are all rational Hodge classes (rational-coefficient Hodge classes) expressible as rational linear combinations of complex algebraic subvarieties of X? I.e., is Hdg^k(X, ℚ) = (image of algebraic cycles)?
- **Importance**: Intersection of topology, complex analysis, and algebraic geometry. Core of algebraic-cycle theory.
- **Current status**: **unresolved**. The case k = 1 (Lefschetz (1,1) theorem) is proven. General k is open. Grothendieck showed the original "generalized Hodge conjecture" to be false (1969).

**(6) Birch–Swinnerton-Dyer Conjecture (1965)**
- **Source**: Bryan Birch · Peter Swinnerton-Dyer, "Notes on elliptic curves I, II", Journal für die reine und angewandte Mathematik 212 (1963), 218 (1965). Based on EDSAC computer calculations from the early 1960s.
- **Question**: Over the rationals ℚ, is the rank of the Mordell-Weil group E(ℚ) of an elliptic curve E equal to the order of vanishing at s = 1 of the Hasse-Weil L-function L(E, s)? Moreover, is the leading coefficient of the Taylor expansion of L(E, s) near s = 1 given by an explicit formula in the arithmetic invariants of E (regulator, size of the Shafarevich-Tate group, Tamagawa numbers, etc.)?
- **Importance**: Connects the rational-point structure of elliptic curves with the analytic behavior of L-functions. Central in arithmetic geometry.
- **Current status**: **unresolved**. Gross-Zagier (1986) + Kolyvagin (1988–1991) prove parts of weak BSD in the rank ≤ 1 case. General rank remains open.

**(7) Poincaré Conjecture (1904) — solved**
- **Source**: Henri Poincaré, "Cinquième complément à l'Analysis Situs", Rendiconti del Circolo Matematico di Palermo 18 (1904), 45–110. Original question: "Is every simply connected closed 3-manifold homeomorphic to the 3-sphere S^3?"
- **Importance**: Foundation of 3-manifold classification. Central problem of 20th-century topology.
- **Solution**: **Grigori Perelman** released three preprints on arXiv in 2002-2003. By adding surgery techniques to Hamilton's Ricci-flow program, he completely proved Thurston's geometrization conjecture and, as a by-product, solved the Poincaré Conjecture. Perelman declined both the 2006 Fields Medal and the 2010 Clay Millennium Prize (US$ 1,000,000). Details in PROB-P0-2.
